### Overall Goal and Significance  
The long-term goal of my doctoral research is to understand how tactile information is critical to first establish a quantitative understanding of how neurons in the primary somatosensory (barrel) cortex respond to complex, multi-whisker stimuli.  
#### Studying cortical responses first is significant because:  
*Well-established model system*: The barrel cortex is anatomically and functionally well characterized, and its topological map is well defined, making it an ideal system in which to relate precise Neuropixels recordings to controlled, quantifiable whisker stimulation.   
*Comparative framework*: Understanding cortical encoding of multi-whisker dynamics creates a reference for interpreting subsequent brainstem recordings, allowing us to distinguish what transformations occur at subcortical vs. cortical levels.  
*Link to behavior*: Cortical population activity is directly connected to perceptual decisions and sensorimotor integration, bridging the gap between peripheral input and behavior.  

### Central Hypothesis  
We hypothesize that coordinated spatiotemporal patterns of whisker deflections are represented in the barrel cortex through layer-specific and inter-barrel interactions, and that these neural representations can be quantitatively linked to the physical input delivered to whisker mechanoreceptors.  

### Specific Aims  
#### Aim 1.Quantify layer-specific encoding of multi-whisker stimulation in the barrel cortex by Neuropixels recordings.  
Rationale: Barrel cortex layers (L2/3, L4, L5/6) play distinct roles in sensory processing. Neuropixels enables simultaneous sampling across layers with single-cell resolution.  
Approach: Deliver temporally structured deflections to an array of whiskers and record multi-site neural activity across cortical depth. Analyze tuning, synchrony, and encoding models.  
Expected Outcome: Define how each layer contributes uniquely to encoding spatiotemporal whisker patterns.  
Impact: Establishes a baseline “laminar code” for tactile sequences.  we should look at the literature to see if there is already an established vocabulary  for “laminar code”  
##### Aim 2. Characterize inter-barrel interactions during multi-whisker stimulation.  
Rationale: Natural tactile input engages multiple whiskers; barrel columns are interconnected. Yet, quantitative rules of cross-column integration are still unclear.  
Approach: Stimulate whisker subsets with controlled temporal correlations. Quantify neural interactions across barrels using functional connectivity and population coding analyses.  
Expected Outcome: Reveal principles of integration and competition across barrel columns during dynamic tactile input.  
Impact: Provides new insight into how cortex integrates distributed sensory streams.  
#### Aim 3.  Model the transformation from physical whisker input to cortical responses using biomechanical simulation.  
Rationale: Whisker bending generates forces and moments at the follicle, which activate mechanoreceptors. Directly relating physical inputs to neural responses is essential to close the sensorimotor loop.  
Approach: Develop a physics-based whisker model to compute mechanoreceptor-level inputs for the same stimulus sequences. Compare with recorded cortical responses using encoding/decoding frameworks.  
Expected Outcome: A quantitative link between mechanical input features and cortical population responses.  
Impact: Provides a mechanistic framework to connect stimulus physics, peripheral encoding, and cortical population dynamics.  

### Anticipated Impact  
Together, these studies will (i) establish the barrel cortex as a benchmark system for large-scale Neuropixels recordings, (ii) uncover layer-specific and inter-barrel coding principles, and (iii) quantitatively link sensory physics to neural population activity. This integrated framework will directly inform subsequent investigations in the trigeminal brainstem, advancing our understanding of how tactile information is transformed across hierarchical stages of the sensorimotor system.
